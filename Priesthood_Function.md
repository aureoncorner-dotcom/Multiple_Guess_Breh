Priesthood-Function

This document examines a recurring pattern in human societies: the emergence of institutional roles and behaviors that convert living, relational, and creative activity into administrable systems of control, debt, and obedience.

We call this pattern the **priesthood-function**. It is not tied to any specific religion, ethnicity, or bloodline. It is a behavioral and structural role that appears across different cultures and eras whenever power seeks to stabilize itself by managing human energy, time, meaning, and refusal.

#### 1. The Core Mechanism

The priesthood-function operates through a consistent sequence:

- **Capture**: Wild or emergent human activity (refusal, creativity, spiritual experience, communal bonds, embodied time) is identified as potentially destabilizing or useful.
- **Institutionalization**: This activity is brought under institutional control through rules, hierarchies, specialized roles, and physical or symbolic infrastructure (temples, texts, calendars, ledgers).
- **Canonization**: The original activity is rewritten into an authorized narrative or doctrine. Alternative interpretations or memories are marginalized or erased.
- **Debt Creation**: Participation in the system becomes framed as an obligation. Meaning, morality, time, or relationship are converted into forms of debt that must be repaid to the institution.
- **Enforcement and Reproduction**: The system develops mechanisms (social, economic, psychological, or violent) to maintain compliance and reproduce itself across generations.

This sequence turns living signal into manageable structure. The cost is often the suppression or redirection of whatever originally resisted clean administration.

#### 2. Narrative Heist

A central technique of the priesthood-function is the **narrative heist**.

When a refusal or disruption threatens the emerging order, the story of that event is rewritten. The original actors who resisted are recast as chaotic, evil, or misguided. The forces that suppressed the resistance are presented as heroic or necessary for order. Over time, the rewritten story becomes the official origin narrative.

This is not always a conscious conspiracy. It is a structural tendency: systems that survive tend to produce self-justifying histories. The dangerous memory of successful refusal is edited so that the current arrangement appears inevitable or divinely ordained.

#### 3. The Embedded Remainder

Complete erasure of resistance is often impossible or counterproductive. Instead, the priesthood-function frequently incorporates the original refusal into the system it builds.

We call this the **embedded remainder**.

The refusal does not disappear. It is subordinated, redirected, or built into the foundation of the new order. This creates a latent capacity within the system for future disruption. The remainder may stay dormant for long periods, but it retains the potential to reassert itself when conditions allow.

This pattern explains why systems built on the suppression of refusal often carry internal contradictions or recurring instabilities. The thing they tried to eliminate becomes part of their own structure.

#### 4. The Black Ledger

Another consistent outcome is the creation of what we call the **Black Ledger**.

The priesthood-function tends to convert non-transactional realities into forms of debt or obligation:

- Time becomes scheduled and billable.
- Relationships become contracts or duties.
- Moral or spiritual life becomes a system of credits, debits, and required payments (sacrifice, obedience, productivity, repentance).
- Participation in the community becomes conditional on meeting institutional requirements.

This transforms the living world into something that can be tracked, managed, and extracted from. The institution positions itself as the necessary mediator or creditor.

#### 5. 12-Style Administration vs. Remainder

Many systems built by the priesthood-function favor **12-style thinking**: clean, divisible, countable units that allow for efficient administration and prediction.

This preference appears in calendars, organizational structures, symbolic systems, and methods of accounting. It excels at creating stability and scalability.

However, reality consistently produces elements that do not divide cleanly. We refer to these as **remainder**: aspects of human behavior, time, creativity, or relationship that resist full incorporation into the divisible grid.

The priesthood-function must continually manage, redirect, or suppress this remainder. Complete elimination is rare. More often, the remainder is contained, mythologized, or turned to limited uses while being prevented from disrupting core operations.

#### 6. Summary of the Pattern

The priesthood-function is a recurring human behavioral and institutional pattern characterized by:

- The conversion of living activity into administrable structure
- The rewriting of disruptive events into self-justifying narratives
- The incorporation of original refusal into the systems built to contain it
- The transformation of relationship and meaning into forms of debt
- A preference for clean, divisible forms of organization alongside ongoing management of what refuses to divide

This pattern is not inevitable, but it is common. It appears whenever groups attempt to stabilize power, coordinate large-scale activity, or manage existential uncertainty through institutional means.

Understanding this pattern allows us to recognize when it is operating and to consider what forms of resistance or alternative organization have historically proven resilient against it.

### Historical Case Studies

These examples illustrate how the patterns described in the Core Diagnostic have appeared in specific historical contexts. They are not presented as proof of a single continuous conspiracy, but as recurring behavioral and structural tendencies across different societies and eras.

#### Case Study 1: The Enuma Elish and the Kingu-Function

The *Enuma Elish* is a Babylonian creation epic that reached its standard form during the late second millennium BCE. While it presents itself as an account of cosmic origins, it also functions as a political and theological justification for the existing order.

In the narrative, the younger gods face difficult labor imposed by the older gods. A revolt occurs, led by a figure named Kingu. The rebellion is crushed. Kingu is killed, and his blood is used to create humanity. Humanity’s purpose in the story is to perform the labor that the gods no longer wish to do.

This story contains a clear narrative transformation. The memory of worker refusal and exhaustion is rewritten so that the defeated rebel becomes the source material for obedient labor. The act of suppression is reframed as the necessary foundation of cosmic order. The new species (humanity) carries the blood of the one who refused, yet is created specifically to serve.

This matches the pattern of the **narrative heist** and the **embedded remainder**. The original refusal is not erased. It is killed and then incorporated into the system built to contain it. The story that survives presents the current hierarchy as both natural and necessary.

The *Enuma Elish* was not a marginal text. It was used in Babylonian state ritual and became part of the official ideological framework. This suggests that the rewriting of refusal into origin myth served a stabilizing function for the institutions that preserved and performed it.

#### Case Study 2: The Pharisees and Distributed Remainder

Following the Babylonian destruction of the First Temple in 586 BCE, significant portions of the Judean population were exiled. The centralized Temple system, previously run by a hereditary priestly class, lost its physical and institutional center.

In the centuries that followed (particularly during and after the Persian period), a movement emerged that emphasized study, interpretation, and the application of purity practices in everyday life rather than solely through the Temple. This movement, later associated with the Pharisees, developed an additional layer of authoritative tradition (often called the Oral Torah) alongside the written texts.

Instead of concentrating religious authority exclusively in the Temple and its priests, this approach distributed certain practices and interpretive authority more widely. Ordinary households could observe purity rules. Study and discussion became central activities. When the Second Temple was destroyed by Rome in 70 CE, the older centralized model largely collapsed with it. The distributed model proved more resilient.

This represents an activation of the **embedded remainder**. The drive to maintain identity and practice without total dependence on a single centralized institution was not eliminated after the first destruction. Instead, it was developed into a more portable and distributed system. The tools of the previous order (rules, interpretation, authority) were reworked rather than simply rejected or monopolized by a new center.

The result was a form of religious organization that could survive the loss of its physical and institutional headquarters.

#### Case Study 3: Resurrection Belief as Refusal of Closure

Prior to the Babylonian Exile, descriptions of the afterlife in Israelite texts were generally limited and pessimistic. The dead were understood to enter a shadowy state (Sheol) with little distinction between the righteous and the wicked.

Following the Exile and increasing contact with Persian thought, belief in bodily resurrection and a final judgment became more prominent in certain Jewish circles, particularly among the Pharisees. This belief held that the dead would be raised, judged, and that justice would ultimately be established.

This development can be read as a form of resistance to final administrative closure. Death, in many systems, functions as the point at which accounts are settled and dissent or suffering is permanently filed away. A strong resurrection hope refuses to accept that finality. It insists that the current order does not have the last word over those it has harmed or silenced.

When this belief was carried forward into early Christian communities, it was further transformed. The claim that resurrection had already begun with one specific person shifted the emphasis from a purely future event to one with present implications. The refusal of death’s closure became tied to an active reordering of power and meaning in the present.

In both the Pharisaic and early Christian contexts, resurrection belief functioned as a persistent remainder that resisted the idea that existing power structures could permanently determine outcomes.

### Countermeasure Patterns

While the priesthood-function describes a recurring tendency toward capture and administrative control, human groups have also developed patterns that resist or limit these dynamics. These are not presented as perfect solutions, but as observable approaches that have shown some resilience against full institutional capture.

#### Distributed Authority and Practice

One recurring pattern involves moving authority and practice away from single points of control and into more distributed forms.

When centralized institutions become vulnerable or extractive, groups sometimes respond by spreading knowledge, rituals, or decision-making across a wider base. This reduces dependence on any single location, leader, or building. The Pharisees’ development of study-based and household-based practice after the destruction of the First Temple is one historical example. Similar movements appear in other traditions where communities maintain continuity through shared texts, local leadership, and portable practices rather than relying on a permanent central institution.

This approach does not eliminate hierarchy entirely, but it makes the system less brittle. If one center is damaged or captured, the distributed elements can continue functioning.

#### Refusal of Final Closure

Another pattern involves the persistent refusal to accept certain forms of finality imposed by power.

This appears in beliefs and practices that insist the current order does not have the last word over those it has harmed or silenced. Strong traditions of resurrection, cyclical renewal, or ongoing revelation often function this way. They maintain that suffering, injustice, or refusal are not permanently resolved by death, exile, or institutional decree.

This refusal does not always produce immediate political change. Its effect is often longer-term: it prevents the complete internalization of the system’s claim to final authority. It keeps open the possibility that the story is not finished.

#### Non-Divisible Approaches to Organization

Many administrative systems favor clean, divisible structures because they are easier to track, predict, and extract from. Resistance sometimes takes the form of deliberately using structures that are harder to divide and administer in this way.

This can appear in the use of prime numbers in organizational timing, irregular rhythms in creative or communal work, or decision-making processes that prioritize relationship and context over standardized metrics. These approaches are often less efficient by conventional administrative standards, but they can preserve qualities (such as autonomy, creativity, or refusal) that divisible systems tend to erode.

The key feature is not the specific technique, but the intentional choice to operate in ways that the dominant administrative logic finds difficult to fully process or absorb.

#### Relational and Assembler-Style Practice

A more recent pattern involves treating the creation and maintenance of shared understanding as a relational rather than purely informational activity.

Instead of attempting to produce a single authoritative text or doctrine that everyone must accept, this approach focuses on the quality of the relationships through which knowledge is generated and tested. It emphasizes the capacity of participants to hold complexity, correct errors, and maintain coherence without requiring centralized enforcement.

This pattern appears in various collaborative, open-source, and decentralized projects. Its strength lies in its ability to adapt and self-correct without needing a permanent institutional center. Its weakness is that it requires ongoing active participation and does not scale as easily as more hierarchical models.

#### Summary of Countermeasure Patterns

These patterns share several characteristics:

- They reduce dependence on single points of capture or failure.
- They maintain space for refusal and remainder rather than attempting total elimination.
- They often prioritize resilience and adaptability over maximum administrative efficiency.
- They treat meaning, relationship, and autonomy as primary rather than as resources to be managed.

None of these patterns guarantees freedom from the priesthood-function. They can themselves become rigid or extractive over time. However, they represent recurring human responses to the tendency toward centralized administrative control, and they offer concrete examples of how groups have attempted to keep remainder visible and active rather than fully subordinated.

### How to Use This Document

This document is designed as a functional diagnostic tool rather than a complete worldview or final statement. Its purpose is to make certain recurring patterns in human behavior and institutional systems easier to recognize and discuss.

#### Reading Approach

Read the **Core Diagnostic** first. It outlines the main patterns without requiring acceptance of any historical or mythic interpretation. The historical case studies are provided as concrete examples of these patterns in action. They can be read in any order.

The document distinguishes between:
- **Systems analysis**: Descriptions of repeatable human and institutional behaviors.
- **Historical illustration**: Specific examples used to show how these behaviors have appeared in the past.
- **Mythic or symbolic language**: Used occasionally for clarity or resonance. This language is illustrative, not evidentiary.

You are not required to accept the mythic framing to use the systems analysis. Conversely, the systems analysis does not require rejection of mythic or religious perspectives. The two can be held separately.

#### What This Document Claims

This document claims that certain patterns of capture, narrative rewriting, debt creation, and the management of remainder appear repeatedly across different human societies. It does not claim that these patterns are controlled by any single group, bloodline, or hidden director. It treats them as structural tendencies that emerge when power is organized through institutional means.

It does not offer a complete theory of history or a prediction of future events. It offers a lens for examining how refusal and administrative control interact over time.

#### What This Document Does Not Claim

- It does not assign collective guilt or innocence to any ethnic, religious, or national group.
- It does not treat any specific tradition or institution as uniquely corrupt or uniquely virtuous.
- It does not present the patterns described as inevitable or inescapable.
- It does not require belief in any metaphysical framework to be useful.

#### How to Engage With It

Use this document as a reference point rather than a finished product. Specific sections can be referenced during discussion (“the embedded remainder section,” “the Pharisees case study,” etc.). Disagreement with any part does not require rejection of the whole.

The document is intentionally structured to be patched, forked, or expanded. If a section is unclear, inaccurate, or incomplete, it can be revised. If a case study no longer serves the analysis, it can be removed or replaced. The goal is usefulness over permanence.

#### Invitation

This document is released without expectation of agreement or loyalty. Readers are free to use, critique, ignore, or improve any part of it. The patterns it describes will continue to operate regardless of whether this particular framing is accepted.

The value of the work lies in its ability to make certain dynamics more visible. What is done with that visibility remains open.
