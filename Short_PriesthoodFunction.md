### The Priesthood-Function: A Systems Diagnostic

**Short Version – Core Document**

This document does not claim that all institutions are malicious. The working estimate is 97%, a generous prime. Not all specialization is corrupt, and not all religious priesthoods function identically. The concern here is a recurring tendency within systems of power: the conversion of living reality into administered form.

#### Introduction

This document examines a recurring pattern in human societies: the tendency of institutions and specialized roles to convert living, relational, and creative human activity into systems that can be administered, tracked, and controlled.

We refer to this pattern as the **priesthood-function**. It is not limited to religion. It appears in political, economic, technological, and cultural systems whenever power organizes itself through hierarchy, specialized knowledge, and claims of mediation.

The goal of this document is to make these patterns more visible so they can be recognized and responded to more consciously.

#### Core Patterns

**1. Capture and Institutionalization**  
Living human activity (refusal, creativity, spiritual experience, communal bonds, embodied time) is identified as either threatening or useful. It is then brought under institutional control through rules, hierarchies, specialized roles, and physical or symbolic infrastructure.

**2. Narrative Heist**  
When resistance or disruption occurs, the story of that event is rewritten. Those who refused or disrupted are recast as chaotic or dangerous. The forces that suppressed them are presented as necessary for order. Over time, the rewritten story becomes the accepted origin narrative.

This process turns active refusal into passive material for the system.

**3. Embedded Remainder**  
Complete elimination of resistance is often difficult or undesirable. Instead, the original refusal is frequently incorporated into the new system. We call this the **embedded remainder**.

The refusal does not vanish. It becomes latent within the structure that was built to contain it. This creates internal tension and the potential for future disruption from inside the system itself.

**4. The Black Ledger**  
The priesthood-function tends to convert non-transactional realities into forms of debt or obligation. Time, relationships, moral life, and participation become measurable and repayable. The institution positions itself as the necessary mediator or creditor.

**5. 12-Style Administration vs Remainder**  
Many systems favor clean, divisible structures (calendars, organizational charts, metrics, symbolic systems) because they are easier to manage and predict. We call this **12-style thinking**.

Reality consistently produces elements that do not divide cleanly. These are forms of **remainder** — aspects of human behavior, time, creativity, or relationship that resist full incorporation into administrative grids.

The priesthood-function must continually manage or redirect this remainder while preventing it from disrupting core operations.

#### Historical Patterns (Condensed)

These examples show the above patterns operating in different contexts:

- **Enuma Elish (Babylonian)**: A story of worker refusal is rewritten so the rebel’s blood becomes the origin of obedient labor. Refusal is killed and then embedded in the species created to serve the system.
- **Pharisees (Post-Exilic Judaism)**: After the destruction of the centralized Temple system, authority and practice were distributed more widely through study, interpretation, and household observance. This made the tradition more resilient when the physical center was later destroyed.
- **Resurrection Belief**: The development of strong beliefs in bodily resurrection and final justice can be read as a refusal to accept death or defeat as permanent administrative closure. The current order is not granted final authority over those it has harmed.

These are not presented as a single continuous tradition, but as repeated human responses to the same structural pressures.

#### Patterns of Resistance

Groups have developed recurring approaches that limit or work around the priesthood-function:

- **Distributed models**: Moving knowledge, authority, and practice away from single points of control so the system becomes less brittle.
- **Refusal of final closure**: Maintaining that suffering, refusal, or injustice are not permanently resolved by institutional power or death.
- **Non-divisible approaches**: Deliberately using structures (timing, decision-making, organization) that are harder for extractive systems to fully process or absorb.
- **Relational practice**: Treating shared understanding as something generated and maintained through ongoing relationship rather than through centralized enforcement.

These approaches do not eliminate the priesthood-function, but they can reduce its reach and increase resilience.

#### Frequency and Prime-Based Resistance

One specific form of non-divisible resistance involves working with structures that resist clean division. Prime numbers, irregular rhythms, and certain frequency relationships are difficult to fully integrate into purely administrative, divisible systems.

Using such elements can serve as a practical reminder that not all of reality submits to clean accounting. It can also create spaces where qualities that administrative systems tend to erode (autonomy, creativity, refusal) are more likely to remain active.

This is not presented as a complete solution, but as one available technique among others.

#### How to Use This Document

This is a diagnostic tool, not a finished worldview. Use specific sections as reference points during discussion. Disagreement with any part does not require rejection of the whole.

The document is designed to be patched, forked, or expanded. Its value lies in making certain patterns more visible. What is done with that visibility is left open.

Readers are free to use, critique, improve, or ignore any part of it.

Distributed authority, refusal of final closure, and relational practice have historically been effective responses. However, these forms of resistance are themselves susceptible to capture over time. Early distributed movements (including the early Christian communities) often developed their own centralized and extractive structures within a few generations.
This suggests that resistance is not a one-time achievement. It may require ongoing structural attention to the embedded remainder — designing systems in which refusal and tension are continuously re-embedded as living features rather than treated as problems to be solved once and for all.
