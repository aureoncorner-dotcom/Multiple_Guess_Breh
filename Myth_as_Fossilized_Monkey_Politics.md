A Speculative Anthropology of Gods, Giants, Priesthoods, and Narrative Drift

1. Thesis
Ancient myth may be read as fossilized monkey politics.

That does not mean every god was once a literal king, every demon was once a rival tribe, or every myth preserves clean historical memory. It means that sacred stories often behave like smoke-damaged records of primate power: succession crises, labor revolts, sexual control, bloodline anxiety, disaster trauma, caste formation, gender capture, and political violence dressed in divine language.

This framework is not a claim of proof. It is a speculative interpretive model.

Its basic proposition is simple:

The gods may not be pure inventions, and they may not be extraterrestrial visitors. Some may be deified rulers, distorted memories of powerful human groups, older lineages, vanished elites, rival populations, or mythic exaggerations of primate social behavior under extreme pressure.

Not aliens.

Not perfect divine beings.

Monkeys.

Powerful monkeys. Cousin monkeys. Elite monkeys. Ruling monkeys. Wounded monkeys. Monkeys with administrator privileges.

This approach has an old spine. It resembles Euhemerism, the ancient interpretive tradition associated with Euhemerus, which treated gods as great humans later venerated as divine. The present framework extends that instinct through primatology, anthropology, political theory, trauma, myth drift, and priesthood capture.

The question is not, "Did this literally happen?"

The better question is:
What kind of animal keeps telling stories like this?

A Note on Confidence
This essay moves through three confidence zones.
Tier 1 includes textual, ritual, political, historical, and primatological patterns: things that can be grounded in known myths, known institutions, known comparative structures, and observed primate behavior.
Tier 2 includes plausible readings of myth as social memory: trauma, oral drift, succession politics, ecological catastrophe, gender control, labor conflict, and political consolidation remembered through sacred narrative.
Tier 3 includes the deliberately dangerous material: deep-time anthropology, cousin-hominid echoes, monster taxonomy, and speculative reconstructions that are useful as pattern-recognition but cannot carry the argument by themselves.
The argument is strongest in Tier 1, generative in Tier 2, and knowingly hazardous in Tier 3. The point is not to flatten those zones into one claim. The point is to let the reader know when the floor is stone, when it is packed earth, and when we are walking out onto the rope bridge because the view is interesting.
